Critique Week

by Annelies Kamen, MFA, Printmedia 2014

Critique week is coming up! And you can find the schedule here: [link to PDF]. But you know, there’s really no reason for you to freak out about it. Think about it this way: except, perhaps, for a relatively brief instant, that crit week is not about you. If you’re prone to critique anxiety this will be a welcome thought. If not, you can savor your moment under the microscope and still take advantage of the crit week’s real wealth of value: other people’s critiques. Sitting in on or participating in your colleague’s critiques can be an inestimable source of new perspectives, methods of working and thinking, material knowledge and tools for discussion. Hearing the ways in which your fellow makers describe and defend their practice not only provides you with new resources to bring to the studio, but also strengthens conversation and relationships within the community of artists you are building for the rest of your career.

Of course, your critique and the conversation about your practice that ensues are important as well, and you should prepare in a way that allows you to get the most useful feedback from your panel. I find it helpful to spend some time the day before my critique writing; if the words I’d like to use to talk about my work exist somewhere on paper, I’m less likely to get tongue-tied during the crit. Don’t hesitate to direct the panel to your areas of concern, and away from conversations that feel tangential. If you conceptualize your critique as a tool rather than as an evaluation, you’re more likely to get the specific and directed responses necessary to develop your practice, and you’ll be calm enough to head to your neighbor’s critique next.

New Courses and Faculty

One of the many features of the monthly newsletters, especially around registration time, will be a spotlight on new courses and new faculty. We will profile exciting additions to the SAIC curricular landscape whenever we can so you can keep an eye out for seminars and advisors that are of interest. Below are some faculty who will be teaching in the Interdisciplinary category by invitation of the Dean.

Amy Yoes

It only took 30 years, but SAIC has brought BFA alumna, Amy Yoes, back to Chicago. Along with her husband, Jorge Colombo (who was the Art Director of NewCity in the 1980s and is a well-known graphic designer, illustrator, and photographer), Amy will be in residence with us for the Fall of 2014. Along with teaching in the CP program, Amy is offering an Interdisciplinary Graduate Seminar and will be Advising ten students (there are some slots left!) Amy’s practice is multi-faceted, cross-disciplinary, and cross-medial. She is happy to advise across design and studio disciplines as her work has long involved architectural installation, photography, video, painting, and sculpture. An interest in decorative language and architectural space permeates all of her projects as she responds to formal topologies of ornament and style that have reverberated through time.

Kirsten Leenaars

We are excited to welcome Kirsten Leenaars to our pool of advisors for next semester. Kirsten is full time faculty in Contemporary Practices as well as teaching in Performance and Arts Administration and Policy. As a self-described “collector of personal stories,” Kirsten’s social practice oriented works use video and performance to research and understand our ways of being. She often interacts with specific communities, incorporating them into her projects in order to explore the nature of human interactions that are professional and personal, real and fictional. She was educated at the Rietveld Academy in Amsterdam, the Piet Zwart Institute in Rotterdam, and in Chicago at UIC.

Andy Yang

We are also thrilled to welcome Andy Yang into the advising pool from the Liberal Arts department at SAIC. With a PhD in evolutionary biology from Duke University and an MFA in Visual Arts from Lesley University, Andy’s expertise is in the uncertain spaces of nature and culture, with a particular interest in ecology, evolutionary biology, and the history and philosophy of science. For artists who have a research-based practice and an interest in visual studies, writing, or material culture, Andy’s experience as a Research Associate at the Field Museum of Natural History and as a published writer in Antennae, Biological Theory, Gastronomica, International Studies in the Philosophy of Science, and City Creatures will be enormously helpful. Able to advise across sculpture, photography, video, multimedia installation, and writing, Andy welcomes students from across the school.

Katarina Weslien

Katarina is a multi disciplinary artist, educator and publisher who has previously taught at SAIC as a visiting artist in the Fiber and Material Studies department. Her work takes form in cross-media installation, immersive experiences, collaborative efforts, video and photography to address issues relating to the fragility of cultural memory, imagination and radical shifts of perspective. She is the Editor of the Moth Press at the Maine College of Art in Portland, publishing interviews with Alison Knowles and Marina Abramovic, as well as editing Mapping The Intelligence of Visual Work by Anne West. With Joan Livingstone she has co-lead numerous study trips to India for SAIC, focusing on material culture, pilgrimage studies and contemporary art.

New team-taught course: Media Future: Digital Heritage and Policy

Led by Shawn Decker and Adelheid Mers

Excitement abounds in museum contexts to employ digital media to preserve and record resources, create new user interfaces, remote access and smart didactics, support and expand scholarship through effective databases, connect with audiences of different abilities, develop new approaches to metrics, update marketing and development tools and strategies, including social networking, and also to present new media artwork in appropriate ways. Those complex concerns, discussed most recently under the heading of Digital Heritage, are unfolding within the larger framework of Electronic Cultural Policy. The development of national information regulatory systems like the Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) and other, global efforts to further expand legislation that supports digital rights management technologies are setting copy-right driven creative industries at odds with the cultural democracy museums seek to enable as part of their mission. Through critical readings accompanied by student driven administrative and artistic hands-on projects, this course will explore an environment in which the development of artistic and institutional mediascapes, including abilities to sample and create mash-ups, is predicated on the preservation of consumer autonomy.
