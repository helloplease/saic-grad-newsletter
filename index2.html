Friends!

Hey guys and gals,

We are excited to present to you our first Grad News Letter! This letter is intended to share with you, in an informal fashion, news and views from across the grad division. Alas, we are too occupied with all those endless papers, parties, shows, projects, and what not to know what’s going on. Well, I hope this new monthly newsletter will cater to our needs to make this blobby/massive/exciting/awesome institution a bit more transparent. If you have suggestions for topics, other pressing thoughts, or fun illustrations, I’d love to hear from you! (Fabienne: fzuijd@saic.edu.)

For this issue I’d like to thank our contributors Annelies Kamen (Printmedia 2014),and of course, Graduate Dean Rebecca Duclos. Without Rebecca’s endless efforts, passion, and creative brain this newsletter would not be here. A big big, big thank you!
With this year running to its end, I wish all of us who are graduating this semester the best of luck! For the rest of you: you’re best semester is yet to come…

Critique Week

by Annelies Kamen, Printmedia 2014

Critique week is coming up! And you can find the schedule here: [insert link] But you know, there’s really no reason for you to freak out about it. Think about it this way: except, perhaps, for a relatively brief instant, that crit week is not about you. If you’re prone to critique anxiety this will be a welcome thought. If not, you can savor your moment under the microscope and still take advantage of the crit week’s real wealth of value: other people’s critiques. Sitting in on or participating in your colleague’s critiques can be an inestimable source of new perspectives, methods of working and thinking, material knowledge and tools for discussion. Hearing the ways in which your fellow makers describe and defend their practice not only provides you with new resources to bring to the studio, but also strengthens conversation and relationships within the community of artists you are building for the rest of your career.

Of course, your critique and the conversation about your practice that ensues are important as well, and you should prepare in a way that allows you to get the most useful feedback from your panel. I find it helpful to spend some time the day before my critique writing; if the words I’d like to use to talk about my work exist somewhere on paper, I’m less likely to get tongue-tied during the crit. Don’t hesitate to direct the panel to your areas of concern, and away from conversations that feel tangential. If you conceptualize your critique as a tool rather than as an evaluation, you’re more likely to get the specific and directed responses necessary to develop your practice, and you’ll be calm enough to head to your neighbor’s critique next.
